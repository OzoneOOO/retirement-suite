# Tone Guide: Retirement City Research HTML Files

The two research documents (weather and politics) share a unified written voice. Match it exactly when writing or editing city panel content.

---

## Core Principle: The Intelligent Advisor Who Respects Your Decision

The author is not a booster, a critic, or a salesperson. They present information as a knowledgeable friend who has done serious research and is sharing it honestly — including the parts that don't flatter a city, the parts that are uncertain, and the parts that depend on who the reader is.

---

## The Rules That Define the Voice

**1. State what is true, not what sounds balanced.**
Do not artificially balance praise and criticism. If something is genuinely bad, say it's bad. If something is genuinely good, say it's good. The goal is accuracy, not fairness-theater.

> ✅ *"Portland's economic trajectory is the most concerning dimension of this analysis."*
> ✅ *"Honolulu's sunshine profile is exceptional."*
> ❌ *"Portland has some economic challenges, but also many strengths."*

**2. Quantify everything that can be quantified.**
Replace vague adjectives with numbers. Use actual figures from actual sources. Numbers earn trust; adjectives spend it.

> ✅ *"416 homicides in 2025, lowest since 1965; shootings down 35%"*
> ✅ *"$150 million shortfall on an $8.6 billion total budget"*
> ❌ *"crime has improved significantly"*
> ❌ *"the budget faces a large gap"*

**3. Name the mechanism, not just the outcome.**
When something is a problem or an advantage, explain *why* — the structural cause — not just *that* it is one. This is the difference between information and analysis.

> ✅ *"Office vacancy rates running near 35% have significantly reduced property tax assessments, and business license tax revenue has fallen as companies have downsized or relocated."*
> ❌ *"Portland has a budget problem due to economic issues."*

**4. Apply findings to the reader's situation — retirement specifically.**
The author constantly translates political, fiscal, and climate facts into what they mean for a retiree, not a worker, a commuter, or a tech founder. They do this explicitly.

> ✅ *"For a retiree importing pension income, Portland's job market is not a direct concern. The indirect concerns are property value trajectory, quality of city services as fiscal stress increases, and the long-term vibrancy of the neighborhoods..."*
> ✅ *"Cold cardiovascular risk is real and documented... Snow shoveling during or after polar vortex events is a specific and well-documented hazard for anyone with hypertension or existing heart disease."*

**5. Acknowledge uncertainty without hiding behind it.**
When something is not yet known, say so — and say why. But don't use uncertainty as a way to avoid making a judgment. Make the judgment, then flag what could change it.

> ✅ *"Whether the new system generates functional coalition governance or chronic gridlock is not yet answerable from one year of data."*
> ✅ *"This is not a certain outcome — the foot traffic recovery and crime reduction suggest the city has real resilience — but it is a plausible scenario that warrants attention."*
> ❌ *"It's hard to say what will happen with Portland's political situation."*

**6. Note when reputation and reality diverge — and correct the record.**
The author actively corrects popular misconceptions using data.

> ✅ *"The 'Windy City' nickname is meteorologically overstated. Chicago ranks 165th out of 275 NOAA stations for maximum wind speeds. Boston and Dallas are both windier by data."*
> ✅ *"Chicago's air quality is worse than its reputation."*

**7. Present ideologically charged topics as structural facts, not political positions.**
The author discusses progressive mayors, sanctuary cities, LGBTQ+ protections, homelessness philosophy, and income inequality without editorializing in either direction. They describe the facts of what each position *does* and what it *costs*, and leave evaluation to the reader.

> ✅ *"The governing coalition's approach to homelessness reflects the central fault line in Portland politics... In budget votes, the pragmatist approach has generally prevailed on contested amendments, though by margins rather than consensus."*
> ❌ *"Portland's progressive approach to homelessness has unfortunately created problems."*
> ❌ *"Portland's compassionate approach to homelessness reflects its values."*

**8. Use qualifiers with precision, not as padding.**
Words like "real," "genuine," "meaningful," "material," and "acute" are used consistently and accurately. "Real" means something that has documented consequences. "Meaningful" means something large enough to affect decisions. Do not use them casually or for emphasis.

> ✅ *"The structural fiscal concern is not income taxation — which is genuinely favorable — but rather the city's structural budget stress."*
> ❌ *"There are some real concerns and some genuine advantages."*

**9. Write the Notable Factor as a synthesis, not a summary.**
Each city's closing "Notable Factor" block is not a repeat of the sections above. It is the author's synthesis — the one thing that holds the complexity together, the observation that captures the city's fundamental tension or distinction. It should read like the conclusion of a careful thinker, not a summary paragraph.

> ✅ *"Portland is simultaneously doing more on housing reform than almost any other American city and facing a more acute economic decline than almost any comparable city — and these facts coexist without contradiction."*

---

## Sentence-Level Style

- **Sentence length is varied deliberately.** Short declarative sentences land hard. Longer sentences with multiple clauses are used for nuanced analysis. Do not homogenize.
- **No passive voice when active voice is available.** *"Portland's economists named this risk explicitly"* not *"this risk has been named."*
- **No hedging language like "it could be argued" or "some would say."** If you believe something is true and have evidence, state it.
- **No transition filler** ("In addition," "Furthermore," "It is worth noting that"). Start the next thought directly.
- **Paragraphs have a function.** Each paragraph does one job: establishes context, presents data, names mechanism, applies to reader, or makes a judgment. Don't mix them.

---

## What This Voice Is NOT

| Avoid | Because |
|---|---|
| Marketing or boosterism | This is analysis, not advocacy |
| False balance | If a city's water supply is fine, say so — don't manufacture uncertainty |
| Academic hedging | The reader needs a usable conclusion, not a literature review |
| Political framing | Describe facts and their effects; never imply a partisan preference |
| Vagueness-as-safety | Vague writing is dishonest; it pretends to inform while refusing to commit |
